\begin{abstract}
Robot stacks are often organized hierarchically, but their failure handling is not. A local fault can leak upward through perception, planning, skill execution, and mission supervision until a small physical problem becomes a global mission failure. The first version of this paper argued for explicit containment budgets, but it treated retries as nearly free. A v2 hardening pass showed the missing boundary: larger retry budgets reduce upward propagation but can hide persistent unsafe faults. We therefore introduce \method, a risk-calibrated containment policy that budgets local recovery using fault persistence, diagnosis quality, retry cost, escalation cost, and stale-state risk. A deterministic full-scale benchmark spans 12 task families, 6 hierarchy-depth regimes, 6 fault classes, 6 persistence regimes, 5 observability regimes, 5 cost regimes, and 8 containment policies, yielding 518,400 compact condition rows representing 152,285,184,000 evaluations and 13,705,666,560,000 hierarchy-tick decisions. \method is the best non-oracle policy with utility 0.547, success 0.710, propagation depth 0.255, masked unsafe rate 0.043, and stale-state exposure 0.082. The oracle remains best overall. Fixed two retries remains a strong baseline, while unlimited retry lowers propagation to 0.074 but raises masked unsafe rate to 0.231 and stale exposure to 0.333. The contribution is a benchmark and reporting discipline for hierarchical failure containment, not a universal safety proof or real-robot deployment claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Hierarchical robot systems are attractive because they turn complex embodied tasks into manageable parts. Low-level controllers stabilize motion, mid-level skills solve reusable subtasks, and high-level supervisors coordinate long-horizon behavior. Unfortunately, the hierarchy that organizes behavior can also become a channel through which failure propagates. A grasp slip can trigger repeated local retries, retries can poison world state, stale state can mislead a task planner, and a mission supervisor can eventually make a brittle global decision based on corrupted local evidence.

Most prior work responds with diagnosis, compensation, runtime assurance, or recovery branches. Those tools matter, but they do not make failure propagation a first-class design variable. A system can diagnose a fault and still leak too much detail upward. A recovery branch can succeed locally but hide a persistent fault. A runtime monitor can stop an unsafe action but leave the hierarchy without a calibrated escalation policy.

The original Paper55 argued for containment budgets: each hierarchy level should get a bounded amount of local recovery before a summarized failure token escalates upward. The first simulator supported this idea because retries reduced propagation. But the simulator made retries nearly free. V2 hardening fixed that. Under moderate persistent-fault stress, two retries had the best utility, 0.893. Four and eight retries reduced propagation further but increased masked unsafe faults and lowered utility.

That failure changes the final claim. The paper is not about maximizing local containment. It is about risk-calibrated containment:
\begin{quote}
Robot hierarchies should bound failure propagation while also bounding retry cost, stale-state exposure, and masked persistent-fault risk.
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
First, we preserve the v2 persistent-fault stress as a negative control. Second, we introduce a full-scale deterministic benchmark over tasks, hierarchy depths, fault classes, persistence regimes, observability regimes, cost regimes, and policies. Third, we evaluate immediate escalation, fixed retry budgets, unlimited retry, local exponential backoff, risk-calibrated containment, and oracle containment. Fourth, we show that \method is the best non-oracle policy while fixed two retries remains strong and unlimited retry exposes the over-containment failure. Fifth, we provide generated result artifacts, tables, figures, validation metadata, and a reporting checklist.

\section{Related Work And Novelty Boundary}

Classical distributed systems introduced fault containment as a formal concept: a fault should affect only a bounded neighborhood and recover within bounded time. Self-stabilizing and containment-preserving protocols show that locality of corruption can sometimes be proved. Robotics inherits the intuition but not the assumptions. A robot hierarchy is hybrid, physical, partially observed, and authority-bearing. A low-level fault can corrupt the control flow that interprets state, not merely a local variable.

Fault diagnosis and fault-tolerant control provide methods for detecting, estimating, hiding, or accommodating faults. These methods often ask whether the system can keep operating after a sensor, actuator, or model fault. That is adjacent but not identical to containment. A fault can be compensated locally while still leaking misleading state upward.

Behavior trees, supervisory control, runtime assurance, shielding, and recovery planners provide hierarchical structure and safety interventions. They are natural substrates for containment, but they often optimize for eventual success or immediate safety rather than propagation depth, stale-state exposure, and masked persistent-fault risk.

Multi-robot containment control uses the word containment differently: agents converge to a region or formation. That literature demonstrates that containment is a powerful control objective, but this paper uses containment to mean bounding failure spread through authority levels.

The novelty boundary is therefore narrow. The paper does not claim a new monitor, planner, or theorem. It claims that hierarchical robot systems need a measurable containment budget, and that this budget must be risk-calibrated rather than maximized.

\section{Containment-Budget Model}

Consider a robot stack with levels $\level{1}$ through $\level{H}$, where $\level{1}$ is closest to physical actuation and $\level{H}$ is the mission supervisor. A fault at level $h$ can be handled locally, retried, quarantined, summarized, or escalated. The containment policy decides how much local recovery is allowed before escalation.

Let $P$ be propagation depth: the number of higher levels receiving nontrivial failure state. Let $M$ be masked unsafe state, where local retries make the system appear healthy while a persistent fault remains. Let $R$ be retry effort, $E$ escalation effort, and $S$ mission success. A containment policy should optimize a vector:
\[
    (S, -P, -M, -R, -E, -\mathrm{stale}, \mathrm{precision}).
\]
This vector view matters because propagation alone is not enough. Unlimited retries can reduce propagation by never escalating, but that may be worse than escalation if the fault is persistent.

\subsection{Risk-Calibrated Containment}

\method estimates a risk score
\[
    \rho_h = g(\hat{p}_{persist}, \hat{q}_{diag}, c_{retry}, c_{esc}, r_{stale}, r_{human}),
\]
where $\hat{p}_{persist}$ is persistent-fault risk, $\hat{q}_{diag}$ is diagnosis quality, $c_{retry}$ is retry cost, $c_{esc}$ is escalation cost, $r_{stale}$ is stale-state risk, and $r_{human}$ is human-proximity or safety criticality.

The policy maps risk to a local budget:
\[
    b_h =
    \begin{cases}
    b_{high} & \rho_h \text{ low and diagnosis reliable},\\
    b_{mid} & \rho_h \text{ moderate},\\
    b_{low} & \rho_h \text{ high or stale-state risk high}.
    \end{cases}
\]
The key difference from fixed retries is that the budget can shrink when retries are likely to mask a persistent fault.

\section{Containment Policies}

\paragraph{Immediate escalation.}
This policy escalates every local fault upward. It minimizes retry masking but maximizes propagation and escalation waste.

\paragraph{Fixed one retry.}
This policy allows minimal local containment. It is useful when retry cost is high or faults are often persistent, but it may escalate transient faults too quickly.

\paragraph{Fixed two retries.}
This is the v2 strong baseline. It balances local recovery and escalation in the old persistent-fault stress. The final method must beat it, not ignore it.

\paragraph{Fixed four retries.}
This policy tests moderate over-containment. It often reduces propagation but starts to raise masked unsafe and stale-state exposure.

\paragraph{Unlimited retry.}
This is the unsafe extreme. It makes propagation look excellent by keeping faults local, but it can hide persistent unsafe state.

\paragraph{Local exponential backoff.}
This policy schedules retries more carefully than a fixed budget, but it does not explicitly reason about masked persistent faults.

\paragraph{\method.}
This policy uses diagnosis, persistence, cost, stale-state, and safety risk to choose local retry, escalation, or quarantine.

\paragraph{Oracle containment.}
The oracle observes true fault persistence and unsafe-state risk. It is an upper bound, not a deployable method.

\section{Limitations}

The benchmark is deterministic and simulated. It is not a real robot deployment result. It does not prove safety. It does not replace runtime assurance, fault-tolerant control, behavior trees, formal verification, or diagnosis. The utility weights penalize masked unsafe state and stale exposure strongly, which is appropriate for safety-sensitive robot hierarchies but not universal.

The benchmark also assumes that the system can estimate persistent-fault risk, diagnosis quality, and stale-state exposure. A real robot may estimate these quantities poorly. If the risk estimate is wrong, \method can choose the wrong budget. This is why the final paper is a reporting discipline and benchmark, not a universal policy theorem.

\section{Conclusion}

Hierarchical robot systems need more than recovery. They need failure containment that is calibrated against risk. The final Paper55 result is not that retries are always good. It is that containment budgets should be tuned to persistent faults, diagnosis quality, retry cost, escalation cost, and stale-state risk. In a full-scale deterministic benchmark, \method is the best non-oracle policy, fixed two retries remains a strong baseline, and unlimited retry exposes the masked-fault danger that made the original claim incomplete.

\section{Task Families}

The benchmark includes mobile pick-and-place, in-hand manipulation, tool-use assembly, door or drawer navigation, human handover, warehouse tote retrieval, cable or cloth handling, mobile inspection, constrained insertion, bimanual transport, outdoor navigation with manipulation, and recovery after failed grasp.

These tasks stress different failure pathways. Human handover makes masked unsafe state more costly. Outdoor navigation with manipulation increases environmental uncertainty. Cable and cloth handling make local retries tempting but ambiguous. Recovery after failed grasp directly tests whether a system can contain a fault without hiding it.

\section{Hierarchy Depths}

The hierarchy-depth axis includes two-level controller-supervisor stacks, three-level skill hierarchies, four-level standard robot stacks, five-level mission stacks, six-level multi-skill hierarchies, and mixed-depth adaptive stacks. Deeper stacks create more opportunities for upward propagation. Mixed-depth stacks test whether a fixed budget is too rigid.

\section{Fault Classes}

The fault classes are transient perception fault, actuator saturation, contact instability, stale world model, planner precondition violation, and persistent hardware or calibration fault. Transient faults favor local retry. Persistent hardware faults favor escalation. Stale world-model faults are dangerous because local retries can reinforce incorrect state.

\section{Persistence Regimes}

The persistence regimes are rare transient, low persistent, moderate persistent, high persistent, burst persistent, and adversarial recurring fault. The v2 lesson lives on this axis. As persistence rises, the value of additional retry declines and the risk of masking grows.

\section{Observability Regimes}

The observability regimes are fully observed fault, delayed diagnosis, partial observability, noisy false alarms, and masked stale-state observability. Observability determines whether local containment is a trustworthy decision or a gamble. Masked stale-state observability is hardest because the system may think the fault is handled while stale state persists.

\section{Cost Regimes}

The cost regimes are cheap retry with cheap escalation, cheap retry with expensive escalation, expensive retry with cheap escalation, expensive retry with expensive escalation, and safety-critical retry cost. A fixed budget cannot be optimal in all these settings. Cheap retry encourages local recovery; safety-critical retry encourages early escalation.

\section{Metric Semantics}

Mission success measures whether the hierarchy completes the episode without unresolved or masked failure. Propagation depth measures how far failure state travels upward. Containment rate measures local resolution. Escalation rate measures how often failure is passed upward. Retry attempts measure local recovery effort. Recovery latency measures time spent before resolution. Masked unsafe rate measures hidden persistent unsafe state. Stale-state exposure measures how much outdated state survives containment. Escalation precision measures whether escalation happens when it is actually needed. Diagnosis waste measures unnecessary escalation and false alarms. Utility combines these metrics into an audit score.

\section{Why Propagation Alone Is Not Enough}

The original v1 simulator made propagation the main metric. That was incomplete. Unlimited retry can make propagation almost vanish by refusing to escalate. If the fault is transient, this can work. If the fault is persistent, the robot may keep retrying while unsafe stale state accumulates. A submission-ready containment paper must therefore report masked unsafe rate and stale-state exposure.

\section{Why Fixed Two Retries Is A Serious Baseline}

Fixed two retries was the strongest v2 setting. It remains strong in the full-scale benchmark. The final paper does not dismiss it. Fixed two retries is a useful engineering default when persistent-fault risk is moderate and diagnosis is imperfect. \method improves over it by adapting to cost and observability, not by pretending the baseline is weak.

\section{Why Unlimited Retry Is A Necessary Negative Control}

Unlimited retry is the temptation behind many recovery systems. If local recovery is possible, keep trying. The benchmark shows why this is dangerous. Unlimited retry has the lowest aggregate propagation depth, 0.074, but the highest masked unsafe rate, 0.231, and stale exposure, 0.333. A paper that reports only propagation would incorrectly celebrate it.

\section{Case Study: Transient Perception Fault}

A mobile pick-and-place robot briefly loses a visual detection. A local retry can refresh perception and continue. Immediate escalation would waste supervisory bandwidth. \method should allow local containment here because persistence risk and stale-state risk are low.

\section{Case Study: Persistent Actuator Saturation}

An actuator saturates during constrained insertion. More local retries may repeat the same unsafe command. Fixed four retries and unlimited retry can hide the fault. \method should escalate earlier because persistence and safety retry cost are high.

\section{Case Study: Stale World Model}

A planner believes an object is still on a shelf after it has shifted. Local skill retries may appear to make progress while acting on stale state. This is a classic masked containment failure. A good policy must escalate or refresh state before retrying too long.

\section{Case Study: Human Handover}

Human handover raises the cost of masked unsafe state. A retry that is acceptable in warehouse retrieval may be unacceptable near a person. The task axis includes human proximity so containment policy cannot be one-size-fits-all.

\section{Case Study: Noisy False Alarm}

Noisy false alarms make immediate escalation wasteful. A system that escalates every alarm can overload higher-level planning and degrade mission success. \method uses diagnosis quality and false-alarm risk to avoid unnecessary escalation while still bounding retries.

\section{Policy Equations}

Immediate escalation sets local retry effort to zero and escalation high. Fixed retry policies set a constant budget independent of risk. Unlimited retry sets the budget effectively unbounded. Backoff schedules retries but does not reason directly about stale-state masking. \method uses a risk score:
\[
\rho = \alpha p_{persist} + \beta r_{stale} + \gamma c_{retry} + \delta r_{human} - \eta q_{diag}.
\]
The budget decreases as $\rho$ rises. This is the mechanism that distinguishes risk calibration from fixed budgets.

\section{Utility Design}

The utility score rewards mission success, containment, and escalation precision. It penalizes propagation, escalation waste, retry attempts, latency, masked unsafe state, stale exposure, and diagnosis waste. Masked unsafe and stale exposure carry high penalties because a robot that hides a persistent fault can be worse than one that escalates.

\section{Data-Generating Model}

The deterministic generator maps each factor tuple to fault pressure, persistent risk, diagnosis quality, mask risk, escalation need, retry danger, and hierarchy gain. Each policy then chooses implied local effort, escalation, retry, masked risk, stale exposure, precision, waste, and latency. The formulas are in the runner and are deterministic from factor codes.

The generator is not a physics simulator. It is a mechanism audit. Its purpose is to expose whether a containment policy trades propagation against masked risk and cost in a sensible way.

\section{Real-Robot Extension}

A real extension should instrument a hierarchical robot stack with fault injection and logging at each authority level. It should record low-level controller faults, skill recovery attempts, planner state updates, supervisor escalations, retry counts, stale-state markers, and mission outcomes. It should include transient faults, persistent faults, false alarms, delayed diagnosis, and human-proximity tasks.

The real study should tune immediate escalation, fixed budgets, backoff, risk-calibrated containment, and an oracle-like human-labeled upper bound on training tasks, then evaluate on held-out tasks, fault classes, and diagnosis regimes. It should report the same metrics used here.

\section{What Would Falsify The Claim}

The claim would be weakened if fixed two retries, backoff, or immediate escalation matched \method across persistence, observability, cost, depth, and fault-class stress while keeping masked unsafe and stale exposure low. It would also be weakened if \method won only because utility weights over-penalized masking. A real robot study could falsify the benchmark if masked unsafe state is not measurable or if risk calibration cannot estimate persistence and diagnosis quality.

\section{Claim-Language Guardrails}

Use "risk-calibrated containment" rather than "containment solves failure." Use "best non-oracle in the deterministic benchmark" rather than "state of the art." Say that fixed two retries remains strong. Say that unlimited retry is good on propagation but bad on masking. State that real robot validation remains future work.
